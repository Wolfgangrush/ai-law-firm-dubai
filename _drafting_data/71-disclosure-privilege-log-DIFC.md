## Currency Warning
DIFC privilege framework mirrors English law. Three principal heads: (a) legal advice privilege — communications between client and lawyer for purpose of legal advice; (b) litigation privilege — communications for the dominant purpose of pending or contemplated litigation; (c) without-prejudice privilege — settlement negotiations.

## Template Type
Procedural Document (annexed to List of Documents)

## Structural Skeleton
1. **Heading** — DIFC Courts · case number · parties.
2. **Title** — "Schedule of Documents Withheld on Grounds of Privilege".
3. **Columnar table** for each document:

| No. | Date | Document type | Author | Recipient(s) | General description | Privilege claimed | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | YYYY-MM-DD | Email | [Author] | [Recipient(s)] | Legal advice on [generic topic] | Legal advice privilege | Confidential communication between client and external counsel for the purpose of obtaining legal advice |
| 2 | YYYY-MM-DD | Memorandum | [Author] | [Internal] | Litigation strategy for anticipated proceedings | Litigation privilege | Created when litigation was reasonably contemplated; dominant purpose was preparation |

4. **Declaration** — *"The party claims privilege over the above documents on the grounds stated. The party reserves the right to amend this Schedule if further documents come to light."*

## Drafting Conventions
- **Sufficient description without waiving privilege** — describe subject-matter generically; do NOT reveal privileged content.
- **Date · author · recipients** required for opposing party to assess claim.
- **Document type** — letter · email · memorandum · attendance note · briefing.
- **Privilege type** — be specific.
- **Basis line** — short reasoned ground tied to privilege elements.
- **Waiver risk** — partial disclosure may waive over the whole; *Three Rivers (No 5)* principles applied.
- **In-house counsel** — privilege applies where the in-house lawyer is acting in legal capacity, not business; *Pratt Holdings*-equivalent reasoning.
- **Dominant purpose test** — applied for litigation privilege.
- **Common interest** — preserves privilege among parties with common legal interest (e.g., insurer + insured).
- **DIFC vs Onshore distinction** — DIFC privilege rules apply only to DIFC Court proceedings; onshore Dubai (Mainland) civil-law inquisitorial system has no equivalent privilege regime.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"A party making an application for privilege over documents must give sufficient information to enable the other party to assess the validity of the claim, but without thereby waiving the privilege over the substance of the document." (Common-law privilege principles applied in DIFC, paraphrased.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
The privilege log is the principal battleground in DIFC commercial litigation. Solo practitioners advising corporate clients should establish documented client-team early to preserve privilege. Communications labelled "legal advice" but substantively about commercial decisions fail. The Onshore-Mainland system has no equivalent — practitioners advising clients with parallel mainland exposure must triage privilege analysis per forum.
