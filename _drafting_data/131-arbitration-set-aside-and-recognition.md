## Currency Warning
Set-aside applications under Federal Arbitration Law 6/2018 Article 53 (Mainland seats) or DIFC Arbitration Law 1/2008 Article 41 (DIFC seats). 30-day limitation from notification of award. Grounds limited per UNCITRAL Model Law influence — no merits review.

## Template Type
Application (DIFC) / Petition (Mainland)

## Structural Skeleton

**(A) SET-ASIDE APPLICATION** (إلغاء حكم التحكيم)

1. **Heading**:
   - DIFC seat → DIFC Courts (Court of First Instance).
   - Mainland seat → Dubai Court of Appeal (specialist Arbitration Department).
2. **Title** — "Application/Petition to Set Aside Arbitral Award".
3. **Time-limit** — 30 days from notification of award.
4. **Grounds** (Article 53 FAL / Article 41 DIFC AL — both based on UNCITRAL Model Law):
   - Invalid arbitration agreement.
   - Party's incapacity / lack of legal representation.
   - Party not given proper notice of appointment of arbitrator or proceedings.
   - Award deals with matters outside scope of arbitration.
   - Tribunal composition not in accordance with parties' agreement.
   - Award contrary to public policy of UAE / DIFC.
   - Subject-matter not arbitrable.
5. **Reliefs sought** — set aside award; costs.
6. **Stay of enforcement** — separate urgent application typically.

**(B) AWARD RECOGNITION + ENFORCEMENT**

*DIFC-seated awards* enforced via DIFC Court ratification:
1. Application to DIFC Court for ratification.
2. Ratified award enforced as DIFC Court judgment.
3. Onshore enforcement via DIFC-Dubai Protocol.

*Mainland-seated awards* enforced via Dubai Courts:
1. Application to Dubai Court of Appeal for ratification.
2. Court reviews limited grounds (no merits review).
3. Ratified award enforced through Execution Court.

*Foreign awards (NYC)* enforced via Dubai Courts or DIFC Court:
1. Application supported by:
   - Authenticated original award + arbitration agreement (or certified copies).
   - Arabic translation by court-recognised translator (Mainland) or English (DIFC).
2. Grounds for refusal limited to NYC Article V:
   - Invalid arbitration agreement.
   - Lack of notice / opportunity to be heard.
   - Award beyond scope of submission.
   - Tribunal composition or procedure not in accordance with agreement.
   - Award not yet binding or set aside in seat country.
   - Subject-matter not arbitrable in enforcement state.
   - Public policy.
3. No merits review.

## Drafting Conventions
- **Limited grounds for set-aside / refusal** — both regimes mirror UNCITRAL Model Law / NYC Article V.
- **30-day limitation for set-aside** — strict.
- **Stay of enforcement** pending set-aside — separate application; not automatic.
- **Public policy** — narrowly construed; not a backdoor to merits review.
- **DIFC-Dubai Protocol** — DIFC Court-ratified awards enforced onshore without re-litigation.
- **NYC enforcement** — UAE acceded 2006; foreign awards enforceable in UAE; UAE awards enforceable in 168+ Contracting States.
- **Apostille / legalisation** — for foreign awards, authentication chain required (apostille if foreign court in Hague Convention State; embassy authentication otherwise).
- **Arabic translation** — court-recognised translator for Mainland filings.
- **Currency conversion** — award currency preserved at enforcement.

## Fair-Use Excerpt
"An award rendered in an arbitration shall be recognised and enforced unless one of the limited grounds for refusal is established. The court shall not review the merits of the award. Set-aside applications must be filed within thirty days of notification of the award." (Federal Law No. 6 of 2018 + DIFC Arbitration Law No. 1 of 2008 + New York Convention, synthesised.)

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Arbitration award enforcement is a significant practice area in Dubai given the volume of DIAC arbitrations + foreign awards seeking UAE enforcement. Solo practitioners must triage: (a) DIFC-seated → DIFC Court + DIFC-Dubai Protocol; (b) Mainland-seated → Dubai Court of Appeal Arbitration Department; (c) foreign → NYC framework + relevant court. Set-aside applications face high bar — public-policy grounds narrowly construed; merits not reviewable.
